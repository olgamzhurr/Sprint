Welcome

About This Sprint

You have an idea. Maybe it's been sitting in your head for months-or maybe it just hit you last week. Either way, you're here because you want to turn that idea into something real. This sprint will take you from concept to paying customers in 10 working days.

Most entrepreneurs get stuck in endless planning, building without talking to customers, or waiting until everything is "perfect." This sprint breaks those traps. You'll validate before building, make Go/No-Go decisions with real data, and launch publicly on Day 10-no excuses.

Every successful company started somewhere small. Airbnb began with air mattresses. Spanx started with scissors and pantyhose. The difference between ideas that become companies and ideas that stay ideas is action.

What You'll Create

  • Validated problem with real customer evidence
  • Clear customer persona and positioning
  • Complete business model with pricing
  • Registered legal entity and business accounts
  • Live website at your custom domain
  • Content strategy with marketing channel set up
  • First paying customers or users
  • Compelling 2-minute founder pitch and investment plan
  • 90-day roadmap for continued growth

The Philosophy: We don't just plan-we execute. You'll work alongside others building their businesses-sharing wins, solving problems together, and holding each other accountable. By the end, you won't just have a plan. You'll have a business.

The Permission-less First Sale Mindset: Throughout this sprint, you'll embrace a powerful truth: you don't need everything to be perfect to get your first customer. Your first sale can be manually fulfilled, delivered by you personally, or produced in a friend's kitchen. The goal is proof of purchase, not profit optimization. The most successful founders understand that a messy first sale teaches you more than months of planning. When someone gives you real money for your product or service, you've learned something no market research can tell you. This doesn't mean cutting corners on quality or ethics-it means being resourceful, finding creative ways to deliver value while you build the infrastructure, and treating obstacles as puzzles to solve, not stop signs.

Choosing Your Idea

Many founders arrive with multiple ideas competing for attention-or with a vague sense they want to start something but no clear direction. Before you can validate a problem, you need to choose which problem to validate.

Goal: Generate multiple possibilities, then select ONE idea to pursue for this sprint.

Framework

Three Sources of Ideas

Source Description Example
Pain Problems you personally experience Sara Blakely cutting pantyhose feet
Passion Areas you're obsessed with Gaming, fitness, sustainability
Pattern Trends you've observed in your work "Everyone still uses spreadsheets for X"

Pain-based ideas are often strongest-you deeply understand the problem. Passion-based ideas give staying power. Pattern-based ideas leverage your unique perspective.

Part 1: Problem Validation (30 min)

Finding Problems Worth Solving

The biggest mistake first-time founders make: falling in love with a solution before understanding the problem. Great businesses don't start with "I want to build an app." They start with "I noticed people struggle with X, and it costs them time/money/happiness."

Consider these statistics: 42% of startups fail because there's no market need. The average founder spends 18 months building before discovering no one wants their product. Companies that start with customer problems are 3x more likely to achieve product-market fit.

Key Insight: Solutions can change; problems are durable. Airbnb started with air mattresses but the problem-travelers need affordable, authentic places to stay-hasn't changed in 15 years.

Framework 1

Jobs-to-be-Done (JTBD)

Origin: Clayton Christensen, Harvard Business School

Customers don't buy products-they "hire" them to get a job done. Your task is to understand what job they're hiring for, not just who they are demographically.

Jobs-to-be-Done Framework
Situation
"When I..."
Motivation
"I want to..."
Desired Outcome
"So I can..."
The McDonald's Milkshake Study

McDonald's wanted to sell more milkshakes. Traditional approach: survey customers about flavors and sizes. Sales didn't budge. Then researchers noticed 40% of milkshakes sold before 8 AM to people alone who drove off immediately.

When asked "What job did you hire that milkshake to do?" customers said: "I have a long, boring commute. I need something for my hand. Something that lasts the whole drive." They weren't buying dessert-they were hiring a boring-commute companion. Competitors weren't other milkshakes-they were bananas and bagels.

Key Question: "What job is the customer hiring my product to do?"

Framework 2

The Mom Test

Origin: Rob Fitzpatrick

Your mom will lie to you. So will your friends. Not maliciously-they're being polite. When you ask "Would you use this app?" they say "Yeah, sounds cool!" and never think about it again.

The Mom Test is a set of rules for conversations that reveal truth: Talk about their life, not your idea. Ask about the past, not the future. Talk less, listen more.

Bad Question Why It's Bad Good Question
"Would you buy this?" Hypothetical, easy to say yes "How are you solving this now?"
"Do you think this is a good idea?" Asks for validation "What's the hardest part about [problem]?"
"Would you pay $20/month?" Leading, hypothetical "What have you paid for to solve this?"

Key Question: "What have they actually done (not said) about this problem?"

Problem Quality Test

Not All Problems Are Equal

Criteria Strong Signal Weak Signal
Frequency Happens daily/weekly Happens once a year
Intensity "I hate this" / costs money "It's mildly annoying"
Current Solutions People pay for bad solutions People just tolerate it
Willingness to Change Actively searching for alternatives "It's fine, I guess"

Hair-on-Fire Test: Is this problem urgent enough that people would use an imperfect solution right now? Or is it a "nice to have" that they'll never prioritize?

Case Study Deep Dives
Airbnb Expensive Hotels + Trust Issues

In 2007, Brian Chesky and Joe Gebbia couldn't afford rent. They noticed a design conference was coming to San Francisco and hotels were sold out. The initial "solution" was literally air mattresses in their apartment. The problem-not the air mattresses-is what created a $100B company.

What Made This Problem Great: Frequency (people travel regularly), Intensity (hotels are expensive), Current solutions (expensive or sketchy), Willingness to change (travelers actively searching before every trip).

Spanx The Uncomfortable Undergarment

Sara Blakely spent 7 years selling fax machines door-to-door. Every day, she wore pantyhose and hated how they looked under white pants. She cut the feet off her pantyhose-and realized millions of women had the same problem.

What Made This Problem Great: She experienced it herself (authentic insight), other women immediately recognized the problem (social proof), the existing solutions were universally disliked (low bar to clear).

Calendly The Scheduling Dance

Tope Awotona noticed that scheduling meetings required 5-7 emails back and forth. The problem wasn't "people need more software"-it was "scheduling meetings wastes everyone's time." The simplicity of the problem led to a simple solution: share your availability with a link. Today, Calendly is valued at over $3 billion.

What If Validation Fails? The Pivot Framework

Sometimes validation reveals the problem isn't real or painful enough. This is valuable data, not failure.

Signs You Need to Pivot:

  • Can't find 3 people with the problem
  • People have the problem but won't pay
  • Everyone already has a good solution
  • The problem is too complex for a first business

Pivot Options:

  • Same Problem, Different Customer: Maybe freelancers don't need this, but agencies do
  • Same Customer, Different Problem: Did they mention OTHER problems in conversations?
  • Narrow the Focus: "Small business owners" → "yoga studio owners in cities"
  • Expand the Focus: Maybe the problem is too specific

Theory Brief (30 min)

The Power of Specificity

You can't serve everyone. The most common startup mistake is trying to appeal to everyone and appealing to no one. Successful companies start narrow and expand.

Consider Facebook: it launched exclusively for Harvard students. Then Ivy League. Then all universities. Then everyone. If Zuckerberg had launched for "everyone on the internet" on day one, he would have been competing with MySpace with a fraction of the resources.

Too Broad Just Right Why It's Better
"Everyone who shops online" "Millennial moms who buy organic baby products" You know where to advertise
"Businesses" "Solo consultants making $100-300k/year" You can price appropriately
"People who travel" "Remote workers who travel 3+ months/year" You understand their specific pains

Key Insight: It's better to have 100 users who love you than 10,000 who think you're okay. Those 100 will tell their friends.

Framework

Blue Ocean Strategy

Origin: W. Chan Kim & Renee Mauborgne

Most markets are "red oceans"-bloody with competition. Blue Ocean Strategy says: don't compete. Create new market space by asking four questions:

Four Actions Framework
Eliminate
What can you eliminate that the industry takes for granted?
Reduce
What can you reduce below industry standard?
Raise
What can you raise above industry standard?
Create
What can you create that the industry never offered?
Cirque du Soleil

Eliminated: Animal acts, star performers, three rings
Reduced: Thrills and danger, humor
Raised: Artistic expression, unique venue experience
Created: Theme, refined environment, multiple productions

Result: A new category (theatrical circus), premium pricing, no direct competitors.

Key Question: "What can we eliminate, reduce, raise, or create to escape competition?"

Case Study Deep Dives
Dollar Shave Club The Underserved Segment

Gillette owned the razor market but focused on "more blades, more technology, higher prices." Michael Dubin noticed regular guys didn't care about 17 blades-they wanted cheap, convenient razors.

Blue Ocean Applied: Eliminated expensive R&D and retail distribution. Reduced blade technology (good enough, not best). Raised convenience (subscription). Created viral marketing and $1/month starting price. Unilever bought DSC for $1 billion.

Notion Power Users First

When Notion launched, there were hundreds of note-taking apps. Notion didn't try to compete with Evernote for casual users. They focused on power users who wanted to build their own systems-people underserved by rigid tools.

By focusing on this specific, passionate segment, Notion created advocates who built templates, wrote tutorials, and recruited their entire teams. The company is now valued at $10 billion.

Mailchimp Small Business Love

Email marketing was built for enterprises. Mailchimp said "we're for small businesses"-free up to 2,000 subscribers, simple enough for a bakery owner. This focus on delighting small businesses (a segment enterprises ignored) built a company worth $12 billion when Intuit acquired them in 2021.

Theory Brief (30 min)

Product Definition: What Are You Actually Selling?

Approach: CPO-level strategic product thinking

Before you can price, market, or sell anything, you need absolute clarity on what you're offering. Most founders skip this step and end up with a fuzzy product that's hard to explain and harder to sell. Today, we define exactly what you're building and in what order.

The Product Clarity Question

If a customer asked "What exactly can I buy from you?" could you give them a clear, complete list with categories and prices? If not, you're not ready to sell.

Key Insight: You don't need to build everything on Day 1. The goal is to see the full landscape of what you COULD offer, then strategically choose what to launch with and what to add later.

Framework

Feature Prioritization Matrix

Approach: Data-driven prioritization

Once you've listed all possible features/products/services, you need to prioritize. You can't launch with everything. Score each item to find your MVP (Minimum Viable Product).

Scoring Criteria (1-5 each)

Customer Value: How much do customers want/need this?

Business Impact: Revenue potential, competitive differentiation

Effort/Cost: Time, money, complexity to deliver (lower = better)

Risk: Technical uncertainty, market risk (lower = better)

Priority Score Formula

Priority = (Customer Value + Business Impact) / (Effort + Risk)

Higher score = Higher priority. Focus on high-value, low-effort items first.

Feature Value Impact Effort Risk Score

Example: Online Video Library 4 4 4 3 1.1

Prioritization

MoSCoW Method

Origin: Dai Clegg, Oracle

After scoring, categorize each feature into one of four buckets. This gives you a clear roadmap of what to build now versus later.

MoSCoW Categories

M - Must Have: Core features for Day 1 launch. Without these, you can't operate.

S - Should Have: Important but not critical. Add within first 30 days.

C - Could Have: Nice to have. 60-90 day horizon.

W - Won't Have (Yet): Future vision. Not now, but don't forget them.

Example: Dance Studio MoSCoW

Must Have: 3 beginner classes (Salsa, Hip-Hop, Bachata), online booking, payment processing
Should Have: Class packages (5-pack, 10-pack), private lessons booking
Could Have: Video recordings, merchandise, performance team
Won't Have Yet: Mobile app, international workshops, instructor certification

Case Study Deep Dives
Salsa Studios NYC From 3 Classes to 25

Maria launched her dance studio with only 3 classes: Beginner Salsa on Tuesday, Thursday, and Saturday. She used a simple booking link and Venmo for payment. Within 30 days, she added intermediate levels based on student demand. By month 3, she had 12 weekly classes and a 5-class package. Today she offers 25 classes weekly across 5 dance styles. Lesson: Start minimal, expand based on demand.

ThreadCraft Jewelry 10 SKUs to 200

James launched his handmade jewelry e-commerce with just 10 earring designs. No necklaces, no bracelets, no custom work. He validated which styles sold, then expanded. He added necklaces in month 2 only after earrings proved demand. Gift wrapping came from customer requests. Now he has 200+ SKUs across 4 categories. Lesson: Let customers tell you what to build next.

FitTrack App Free Only, Then Premium

The fitness app launched with only a free tier-basic workout logging and a small exercise library. No AI coach, no premium tier, no social features. They grew to 10,000 users before adding premium. The premium features were built based on what free users asked for most. Lesson: Validate with free, monetize what's proven.

Theory Brief (30 min)

Value Proposition Design

Origin: Alexander Osterwalder, Strategyzer

A value proposition answers: Why should customers choose you over every other option, including doing nothing? It's not a list of features. It's a clear promise of value that a specific customer will receive.

Value Proposition Formula

For [target customer] who [has this problem], [your product] is a [category] that [key benefit]. Unlike [alternatives], we [key differentiator].

Example (Slack): For teams who struggle with email overload, Slack is a messaging platform that makes work communication faster and more organized. Unlike email, we create transparent channels where everyone can see what's happening.

Framework

Value Proposition Canvas

The canvas maps two things: the Customer Profile (jobs, pains, gains) and your Value Map (products, pain relievers, gain creators). The goal is fit-when your value map directly addresses what customers need.

The Canvas Structure

Pains → What frustrates them

Gains → What they wish they had

Value Map

Gain Creators → How you create value

Pricing Strategy

The Pricing Triangle

Price is the most powerful lever in your business. A 1% price increase can translate to 10%+ profit increase. There are three anchors for any pricing decision:

Three Pricing Anchors

COST-BASED

(Your Floor)

What does it cost you? Add margin.

COMPETITION-BASED

(Reference Point)

What do alternatives cost?

VALUE-BASED

Warby Parker Home Try-On Revolution

Buying glasses online seemed impossible-how do you know if they'll fit? Warby Parker's solution: ship 5 frames to your home for free, keep them for 5 days, return what you don't want. No commitment, no risk. This simple UX innovation removed the #1 barrier to purchase and built a $3B company.

Chewy Unexpected Delight

Key Lesson: What looks like excessive generosity in your return policy or support often pays back 10x in word-of-mouth and repeat purchases.

Legal Foundations & Business Setup

Registering a business might feel like bureaucracy, but it serves critical functions: you can't open a business bank account without one, payment processors require it, and it separates your personal and business finances.

Key Insight: You don't need the perfect structure on day one. Most founders start simple (sole prop or LLC) and restructure later if needed. Don't let legal setup slow down customer validation.

Mistakes to Avoid

Common Founder Errors

Over-Engineering Structure

Solo founder incorporated as a C-Corp because "that's what startups do." Spent $2,000 before making $1 in revenue. An LLC would have cost $200 and been fine for years.

Co-mingling Finances

Using personal credit cards for business expenses. Tax time was a nightmare. Fix: Open a business bank account immediately. It takes 30 minutes.

No Co-founder Agreement

Two founders start without discussing equity, vesting, or what happens if someone leaves. Fix: Write an operating agreement covering equity, vesting (4-year with 1-year cliff), decision rights, and exit terms.

This is general information, not legal advice. Consult a lawyer or accountant for your specific situation.

Case Study Deep Dives: Getting It Right
Stripe Right Structure at the Right Time

Patrick and John Collison started Stripe as a simple Nevada LLC in 2010. Only when they began raising venture capital did they restructure as a Delaware C-Corp-which VCs expect. They didn't over-engineer their legal structure before they had product-market fit.

Key Lesson: Start with the simplest structure that protects you. You can always upgrade later when the business demands it.

Patagonia Structure Aligned to Mission

Yvon Chouinard didn't just build an outdoor clothing company-he structured it around environmental mission. Patagonia became a Certified B Corporation and eventually transferred ownership to a trust dedicated to fighting climate change. The legal structure became a competitive advantage, attracting both customers and employees who shared the mission.

Key Lesson: Your legal structure can reinforce your brand and values. Consider B-Corp certification or benefit corporation status if mission matters.

Buffer Radical Transparency from Day One

Joel Gascoigne published Buffer's salary formula publicly and created a transparent equity breakdown for all employees. Every co-founder agreement, cap table update, and financial metric is openly shared. This radical transparency attracted talent who valued fairness and built a culture where difficult conversations about equity happen early.

Key Lesson: The best co-founder agreements aren't just legal documents-they're conversations. Get equity, vesting, and expectations in writing before the business gets complicated.

Regulatory Pathways

Industry-Specific Requirements

Different industries have different regulatory requirements. Understanding your options helps you make informed decisions about timing and approach while ensuring proper compliance.

Industry Typical Requirements Timeline
Food & Beverage Food handler's permit, cottage food license or commercial kitchen 1-6 weeks
Health & Wellness Professional licenses, liability insurance, facility permits 2-8 weeks
Financial Services Money transmitter license, SEC registration 4-12 weeks
E-commerce Sales tax registration, consumer protection compliance 1-2 weeks
Regulatory Pathways Framework

Pathway 1 - Direct Compliance: Apply for all required permits before launching. Best for highly regulated industries.

Pathway 2 - Partnership Model: Partner with an already-licensed entity for initial operations (e.g., use a licensed commercial kitchen).

Pathway 3 - Scope Adjustment: Adjust your initial offering to fit within lower-regulation categories (e.g., cottage food items).

Pathway 4 - Parallel Track: Begin licensing process while simultaneously validating demand. Collect pre-orders while awaiting approval.

Important: Requirements vary significantly by location and industry. When in doubt, consult with a lawyer or industry specialist. Proper compliance protects your business and your customers.

Case Study Deep Dives
TOMS Shoes Story as Business Model

Blake Mycoskie didn't just sell shoes-he sold a story. "One for One" (buy a pair, give a pair) wasn't just a tagline; it was content that customers shared organically. The founder story of discovering shoeless children in Argentina gave people a reason to buy AND a story to tell others. TOMS built a $600M brand with almost no traditional advertising.

Key Lesson: The best marketing is a story customers want to retell. Make your mission shareable.

Glossier Audience Before Product

Emily Weiss didn't start with a makeup line. She started with "Into The Gloss," a beauty blog where she interviewed women about their skincare routines. After 4 years and millions of readers, she asked her audience what products they wanted-then built exactly that. Glossier had 15,000 customers waiting on launch day.

Key Lesson: Build an audience before you build a product. Listen to them, create for them, and they'll buy from you when you're ready.

Liquid Death Comedy in a Commodity Category

Mike Cessario sells canned water. That's it. But Liquid Death's content is so entertaining-skull branding, fake celebrity deaths, heavy metal marketing-that they've built a cult following selling the most boring product imaginable. Their videos get millions of views. The brand personality IS the product differentiation.

Key Lesson: If your product is similar to competitors, differentiate through personality and content. Entertainment value can beat feature value.

Theory Brief (30 min)

Getting Your First Customers

The first 10 customers don't come from marketing. They come from personal outreach, hustle, and doing things that don't scale. Your first customers aren't just revenue-they're learning. Every conversation makes you better at acquiring customers 11-100.

Paul Graham (Y Combinator): "Do things that don't scale." What works for your first 100 customers is completely different from what works for your first 100,000.

How Great Companies Started

Do Things That Don't Scale

Stripe "Collison Installation"

The Collison brothers stood over developers' shoulders and helped them integrate Stripe right then and there. Founder-led sales at its most personal.

Airbnb Door-to-Door Photography

The founders went door to door in NYC taking professional photos of listings. They personally onboarded every early host.

Pinterest 7,000 Personal Emails

Ben Silbermann personally emailed the first 7,000 users. He walked into coffee shops and asked strangers to try the product.

Zappos The Permission-less First Sale

Nick Swinmurn took photos of shoes at local stores, posted them online, and when someone ordered, he walked to the store, bought the shoes at retail price, and shipped them. He lost money on every transaction-but proved people would buy shoes online without trying them on first.

The Permission-less Mindset

Your First Sale Doesn't Need to Be Perfect

Your first sale can be manually fulfilled, delivered by you personally, or produced in a friend's kitchen. The goal is proof of purchase, not profit optimization.

Scrappy Fulfillment Options
If you need... You could...
Product inventory Buy from a competitor or retailer, resell
A commercial kitchen Partner with a local bakery, restaurant, or church
Manufacturing Find an Etsy seller or local craftsperson to produce
Professional equipment Rent by the hour, borrow from a friend, or improvise
A physical location Use a co-working space, pop-up market, or your living room

Remember: Your first sale isn't about making money. It's about answering a question: Will people actually pay for this? Once you have that answer, you can figure out how to do it profitably.

Theory Brief (30 min)

Launch Execution & Product-Market Fit

Launch is not a single moment-it's the start of a journey. Many founders treat launch like a finish line, but it's really the first day of the real work: finding product-market fit.

Key Insight: The best companies have launched multiple times. ProductHunt allows re-launches. You can announce major updates as "launches." Today isn't the only chance.

Framework

The 2-Minute Pitch

Every founder needs to explain their company in 2 minutes or less. You'll use this constantly: at networking events, in investor meetings, when relatives ask what you do.

Section Duration Content
Hook 10 sec "Did you know that [striking fact about the problem]?"
Problem 20 sec "[Target customers] struggle with [problem]. This costs them..."
Solution 20 sec "[Company] solves this by [mechanism]. Unlike alternatives, we..."
Traction 20 sec "We've already [users, revenue, validation]..."
Vision 20 sec "We're building toward [bigger vision]..."
Ask 10 sec "We're looking for [customers/feedback]. Would love to..."
Framework

Product-Market Fit

Origin: Marc Andreessen, Sean Ellis

Product-Market Fit (PMF) is the moment when your product and market click. Sean Ellis quantified it: ask users "How would you feel if you could no longer use this product?"

The 40% Test

If 40%+ say "very disappointed", you likely have PMF.

Superhuman wouldn't launch until they hit 40%. Every week, they surveyed beta users, analyzed who was "very disappointed," built specifically for them, and resurveyed. It took two years, but when they launched, growth was explosive.

Signs of PMF Signs You're Not There Yet
Word of mouth drives growth Constantly convincing people to try
Users complain when things break High churn-people try once, leave
Struggling to keep up with demand Growth only when actively marketing

Before PMF: Focus on learning, not scaling. Talk to every customer.
After PMF: Focus on growth. Double down on what's working.

Case Study Deep Dives
Superhuman The Deliberate PMF Pursuit

Rahul Vohra spent two years in private beta, obsessively surveying users every week with the "very disappointed" question. He segmented users, identified his ideal customer profile, and only built features for the people who loved the product most. When Superhuman finally "launched," they had 40%+ PMF score and a 400,000-person waitlist. Growth was inevitable.

Key Lesson: Launch is not a deadline-it's a milestone you earn by achieving product-market fit. Don't scale what isn't working.

Mailchimp Did You Mean MailChimp?

Mailchimp's brand is weird, playful, and memorable. Their "Did You Mean MailChimp?" campaign featured intentional misspellings-MailShrimp, JailBlimp, KaleLimp-across billboards and digital ads. People talked about it, shared it, and remembered the brand. This playfulness in a boring category (email marketing) made them unforgettable.

Key Lesson: Your launch doesn't have to be a press release. Create something people want to share because it's surprising, fun, or remarkable.

Slack Social Proof at Scale

Slack's launch messaging was simple: "Loved by 8,000 companies including yours." Instead of explaining features, they led with social proof. They collected testimonials obsessively from beta users and featured real companies (not anonymized quotes) in their marketing. The message was clear: everyone else is using this, why aren't you?

Key Lesson: Social proof is your most powerful launch asset. Even one great testimonial from a recognizable person or company beats a feature list.

What's Next

Your 30/60/90 Day Roadmap

Days 11-30: Learn and Stabilize

Goal: Understand what's working, fix what isn't, serve first customers well.

  • Follow up with every launch-day contact
  • Talk to every customer-get detailed feedback
  • Fix critical bugs or issues
  • Implement top 3 customer feedback items
  • Write your first case study or testimonial

Success: 10+ paying customers, positive feedback, clear understanding of improvements needed.

Days 31-60: Optimize and Grow

Goal: Find repeatable customer acquisition and improve your product/service.

  • Identify your top-performing marketing channel
  • Double down on what's working
  • Cut or reduce effort on what's not working
  • Improve onboarding based on where customers get stuck
  • Build a referral or word-of-mouth program

Success: 30+ customers, at least 1 customer referral, positive unit economics.

Days 61-90: Scale or Pivot

Goal: Scale what's working or make strategic changes based on data.

Product-Market Fit Test: Ask customers "How would you feel if you could no longer use this?"

  • 40%+ say "Very disappointed" = You have PMF. Scale aggressively.
  • 25-40% = Getting close. Focus on those customers.
  • Under 25% = Consider pivoting product, market, or both.

Success: Clear answer on product-market fit; growth plan or pivot plan in place.
